One trade, seven tickers

Most Muslim-friendly ETF portfolios hold five funds and one idea. Two years of daily data show which funds actually diversify — and which just repeat each other at different fee levels.

Data: daily closes, Jul 2024 – Jul 2026 · 502 trading days · correlations computed on daily returns

The halal ETF universe has grown to the point where a U.S. investor can fill every major asset-class slot with Muslim-friendly funds: U.S. equity, global equity, technology, gold, and sukuk. That sounds like diversification. Whether it is diversification is an empirical question — and the answer lives in the correlation matrix, not the fund names.

I pulled two years of daily closes for seven funds: SPUS and HLAL (U.S. equity), SPWO and UMMA (global/ex-U.S. equity), SPTE (global tech), GLDM (gold), and SPSK (sukuk — the only U.S.-listed Islamic bond ETF). Then I computed pairwise correlations of their daily returns.

Three findings stood out. First, HLAL and SPUS correlate at 0.96 — they are functionally the same position, so holding both mostly means paying two fees for one exposure. Second, UMMA and SPWO correlate at 0.94, the same redundancy on the international side, except UMMA charges 0.65% to SPWO's 0.55%. Third, the only funds doing real diversification work are the non-equity ones: GLDM (gold) correlates with the equity funds at just 0.17–0.36, and SPSK (sukuk) at 0.15–0.23 — with a fraction of the volatility.

How to read it

Correlation of daily returns runs from −1 (perfect opposites) to +1 (lockstep). Anything above ~0.8 means the two funds are substantially the same bet: when one has a bad day, the other almost certainly does too. Diversification value shows up below ~0.4 — that's where adding the fund actually changes the shape of your portfolio's risk rather than just relabeling it.

Notice what happens when you select only the five equity funds: the matrix glows deep green almost everywhere. That's one risk factor — global equity beta — wearing five different tickers. Now add SPSK and GLDM. The pale and warm cells that appear are the diversification. Two years of data, one picture.

A practical way to use this: pick one fund per role. One U.S. equity fund (SPUS or HLAL — the matrix says it barely matters which), one international fund (SPWO or UMMA — same story, though fees and track record differ), optionally a tech tilt if you want the extra beta, then let GLDM and SPSK do the actual risk-balancing. Owning both funds in a redundant pair adds fees, not safety.

Caveats, because they matter

Two years is a short window that happens to cover a strong equity bull market — correlations tend to rise in crises, so the equity-to-equity numbers here are, if anything, optimistic about diversification. SPSK's ~2% total return reflects the rate environment of this specific period, not the asset class's ceiling. Past correlation isn't a contract; it's a tendency. And none of this is investment advice — it's a description of how these seven funds have moved, so you can make your own call.

Methodology: daily closing prices, Jul 1 2024 – Jul 1 2026, aligned on common trading days (502 observations). Correlations are Pearson coefficients on daily percentage returns. Volatility is the standard deviation of daily returns annualized by √252. Expense ratios as published by fund sponsors, mid-2026.
